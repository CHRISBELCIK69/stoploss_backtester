TSM Bollinger Mean-Revert — Deep Dive
======================================

WHAT IT ACTUALLY DOES (AND WHAT IT DOESN'T)
-------------------------------------------

This is NOT a stop-loss in the traditional sense. It's a take-profit on
mean reversion. The "stop" line that gets drawn on the chart is actually
a moving average — a curve that the price will eventually cross back to.
When it does, you're out.

The hard stop at the bottom is a separate, conventional safety net. So
there are really two exit conditions running in parallel:

                                 ┌───────────────────────┐
                                 │  Price runs up         │
                                 │  ─────────────────►    │
                                 │           │            │
                                 │           ▼            │
   ──────── 20-bar SMA ─── ──────── ←─ Exit when           │
                                 │           price returns │
                                 │           to MA         │
   ............ Hard stop (50% below entry — never moves) │
                                 │  Exit if catastrophe   │
                                 └───────────────────────┘

The strategy assumes you bought a directional move that's likely to
mean-revert. The MA IS the trigger — when price comes back to its
average, the move is exhausted and you cash out.


THE THREE-PHASE LIFECYCLE
-------------------------

Every Bollinger trade has three distinct windows:

PHASE 1: Warmup (bars 0 → warmupBars)

Default warmup is 10 bars (~10 minutes). During this window:

  - The hard stop is the only exit — your $0.50/$0.42 (50% below entry)
    stop holds.
  - The MA isn't computed at all as an exit trigger.
  - The "stop line" displayed on the chart shows the hard stop, not the MA.

This warmup exists for a critical reason: at bar 1, the option might
still be near entry price, which is literally on the MA (the MA is
roughly the average of all recent prices including your entry). Without
warmup, the strategy would exit on bar 1 every single time. The 10-bar
buffer gives the trade room to actually run before mean-reversion logic
activates.

PHASE 2: MA exit armed (bars warmupBars → end)

Once the warmup is past, the strategy looks at every new bar and asks:

    if bar_low <= ma:
        fill = max(ma, bar_low)
        return exit

Two things happen on this check:

  1. bar_low <= ma — the price has touched or crossed back down to the
     MA from above. This is the mean-reversion signal.
  2. fill = max(ma, bar_low) — your fill is the BETTER of the MA price
     or the bar low. If the bar wicked through the MA but closed above
     it, you fill at the MA. If the bar gapped through cleanly, you
     fill at the bar low.

This is the key behavior that distinguishes Bollinger from a normal stop:

  - A normal stop fills you at the worse price (the stop level if
    touched, or the gap-down open if gapped through).
  - A Bollinger MA exit fills you at the better of the two — because
    the MA is a take-profit target, not a danger zone. You want the
    better fill on the way out.

PHASE 3: Hard stop catastrophe (always active)

Throughout both phases, the original hard stop sits at entry × 0.50
(default). It never moves. Its only job is to catch the worst case
where price collapses straight down without ever running up first — so
the MA exit never gets a chance.

If the trade goes immediately wrong, this is what saves you. If the
trade runs up and then mean-reverts, the MA exit fires first and the
hard stop is irrelevant.


THE MA ITSELF
-------------

Every bar after entry, the strategy recomputes:

    window_start = max(0, i - ma_period + 1)
    window = [float(bars[j]['close']) for j in range(window_start, i + 1)]
    ma = sum(window) / len(window) if window else entry_price

A few subtle things going on:

  - Closes only — opens/highs/lows aren't used. The MA is the average
    of the last 20 closes.
  - Rolling, not anchored — the window slides forward with each new
    bar. The bar from 21 minutes ago drops out as the new bar comes in.
  - Self-correcting — if the option price spikes up and stays elevated,
    the MA gradually catches up to the new level. Your "exit price"
    rises with the MA. So the longer you hold, the higher the floor
    you're protecting.
  - Falls through gracefully — if there are fewer than ma_period bars
    available (entered late in the day), len(window) is just whatever
    exists, and the MA is computed over what we have. No errors, no
    warmup blocking.

This is why Bollinger acts like a dynamic trail with memory:

  - Early in the trade, the MA is dragged down by the entry price →
    exit fires near entry → small profit.
  - Mid-trade, after a sustained run, the MA has caught up → exit fires
    further from entry → bigger profit.
  - Late in the trade, if price has been chopping sideways at a high
    level, MA = current price → the next dip exits immediately.


WHY IT PERFORMS WELL ON SPY 0DTE
--------------------------------

In your earlier test on MRVL, Bollinger MR was the best winner at
+$21.80 — better than holding to EOD. Here's the structural reason:

SPY 0DTE options have a very specific behavior:

  1. Morning pop — directional move from open
  2. Midday consolidation — sideways chop near the new level
  3. Afternoon decay — theta accelerates, premium bleeds even if price
     holds

A strategy that exits on mean reversion captures the morning pop cleanly:

  - Enter at open, ride the pop
  - Price plateaus, MA catches up to current price
  - First minor pullback in the consolidation hits the MA → exit before
    theta kicks in

Compare to Profit-lock with default 50% activation: the trade has to
gain 50% before the trail even arms. By the time it arms, the morning
pop is already exhausted, the MA strategy has already exited with
profit, and Profit-lock then trails sideways into EOD eating theta.

That's why on grindy chop days Bollinger consistently outperforms.


WHERE IT DIES
-------------

Three failure modes:

1. Trades that grind down all day with no run-up

   If the contract drifts from $1.20 → $0.80 → $0.40 with no upside,
   the MA tracks the price downward. bar_low <= ma becomes "is the bar
   low below a continuously-falling average?" — usually yes. So you'd
   exit early on the first dip.

   But here's the problem: in the current code, the exit only fires
   when bars_since_entry > warmup. If the bleed starts within the
   warmup window, the hard stop catches you instead. So you eat a 50%
   loss instead of a 20% loss. The strategy can't help on
   directionally-wrong trades.

2. Whippy markets where price oscillates around the MA

   If the option chops up and down across its own MA every few bars,
   the first dip after warmup triggers an exit. You'll often exit at
   break-even or small profit when actually the trade went on to make
   $50 later. The strategy is allergic to chop.

3. Sub-$0.10 contracts

   When the option premium is tiny, MA differences are pennies. A
   $0.07 entry with MA at $0.06 means a 1¢ dip exits you for a flat
   trade. No strategy works well on near-zero contracts, but Bollinger
   fails especially gracelessly.


THE SUBTLE BUG WORTH KNOWING
----------------------------

Look at the hard-stop stopPrice returned in the trace:

    display_stop = ma if bars_since_entry > warmup else stop_price
    trace.append({'time': bar['time'], 'stopPrice': display_stop})

The chart shows the MA as the "stop line" once warmup is past. But
during the warmup, it shows the hard stop. So the stop line on the
chart is doing double duty — first showing one thing, then jumping to
show something else.

This means the chart's stop trace will visibly JUMP at bar warmup + 1,
from the hard stop level (low) up to the MA (much higher). That jump
isn't a real strategy event — it's just a display artifact from the
trace using two different concepts.

If you ever wonder why the Bollinger stop line "teleports up" early in
the trade on the chart, that's why.


TWO SIMPLE IDEAS TO MAKE IT BETTER
----------------------------------

A. Use a "take-profit at MA cross from below" instead of "MA touch"

   Right now the trigger is bar_low <= ma — any wick down to the MA
   exits. But options have noisy bid/ask spreads — a single tick can
   dip below the MA on a quote glitch.

   A cleaner trigger:

       # Require the previous bar to be above the MA AND this bar to
       # close below it
       if prev_close > prev_ma and bar_close < ma and bars_since_entry > warmup:
           return exit at bar close

   This requires a deliberate cross, not a wick. Fewer false exits.

B. Use the upper Bollinger band as a separate trigger (true Bollinger)

   The current implementation isn't actually using Bollinger Bands —
   it's just using the midline (the MA). Real Bollinger Bands also have
   an upper band at MA + 2σ. A common variant:

     - Exit if price touches the upper band (overbought, take profit)
     - Exit if price falls back to MA (mean reversion)

   Adding the upper band gives you two independent exit triggers and
   tends to catch the absolute peak of a run rather than waiting for it
   to reverse all the way back to the midline.

   That's a 4-line addition and would probably make this strategy
   noticeably better on momentum days.


TL;DR
-----

Bollinger MR is a moving-average take-profit with a hard-stop floor.
It works because options after a run-up tend to revert to their recent
average, and the MA naturally rises with the price so your protection
level rises too. The 10-bar warmup is critical — without it, every
trade exits at bar 1. The strategy excels on morning-pop-then-fade days
and underperforms on monotonic trends or chop. The "stop line" on the
chart is actually showing the MA in Phase 2, which is why it looks
weird compared to other strategies.
